Context in paper

We are interested in whether the participants′ persuasion and influencing to solve disagreements indeed contributes into the leakage of membership-relevant information. To this end, we zoom into the training iterations to explore how the participants collaboratively construct the global model and how they act when disagreement arises. Fig. 2 illustrates the performances of model prediction and MIA-FL during training. The global model demonstrates a clear divergence in its prediction accuracy between the member samples and non-member samples immediately after the attack starts (at epoch 30). The prediction accuracy on member samples (olive curves) steadily increases and gradually approaches the training accuracy (magenta curves), while that of the non-member samples starts decreasing rapidly once the attacker misleads the model to the opposite direction regarding these samples (blue curves). This gives clear clues for the attacker to determine whether a sample is used for training by any other participants. The larger the divergence between the prediction accuracy on member samples and the non-member samples (i.e., the gap between the olive and blue curves), the better the attack accuracy can be achieved. Such divergence is the main root cause of the active MIA-FL.

Starting point and attack rounds: We next investi- gate how different starting points of the attack affect the attack performance. We set the starting point at epoch {0, 20, 40, 100, 200, 300} respectively, and observe the perfor- mance of MIA-FL (Fig. 3).

In general, the later MIA-FL starts, the better accuracy can be achieved. If MIA-FL starts at the very beginning of the training (i.e., starting point being 0, magenta curves in Fig. 3), the attack accuracy is significantly low at its first several rounds (from 0 to 20). This is because after the model converges, the model′s performance becomes stable, which is beneficial for the attacker to better distinguish member samples from non-member samples based on the model′s prediction.

Context in paper

We note that the privacy guarantee provided by differential privacy is based on the indistinguishablity of the output of an algorithm that takes inputs of any two individual samples in the dataset. Such privacy promise holds in the federated learning when all participants follow the learning protocol, as the norm of the local gradients (which decides the sensitivity of the algorithm) can be clipped and unified. However, an active attack can simply break the DP privacy guarantee as the feeding of the malicious updates can largely affect the output space of the algorithm.

Therefore, as expected, MIA-FL does not lose any advantage with DP-based federated learning. That is, the attack accuracy of a Grey-box-I attacker in the DP-FL (Table III) is the same as that in plain FL without DP mechanisms (Table II).

Fig. 4. The required attack rounds by MIA-FL

Context in paper

The generalization ability of the model affects the required attack rounds, as shown in Fig. 4 (in which the MIA-FL′ starting point is fixed at epoch 30). Due to the better generalization of the model on the 1000Genome dataset (79.51% test accuracy as reported in Table II), much more attack rounds are required to achieve the same attack accuracy as on the Location30 dataset (64.71% test accuracy). For example, 250 rounds are needed to achieve 80% of attack accuracy on 1000Genome while only around 50 rounds are needed on Location30 (Fig. 4c). This is because when the model generalizes better, the MIA-FL has to take more rounds to reduce the model prediction accuracy on non-member samples.

Targeted MIA-FL: We also explore the performance of targeted MIA-FL, and our experimental results are summarized in Table IV. The process of our targeted MIA-FL consists of two steps, i.e., (1) to conduct non-targeted attack first to classify members from non-members, and (2) based on the samples that are classified as member, the attacker apply Equation 5, 6 and 8 to identify the samples′ owner. We therefore first report the general attack accuracy (i.e., non-targeted) on the member samples in the columns of “Mem”, and the targeted attack accuracy among the true member sample in the columns of “T_mem”, and the the targeted attack accuracy among the total predicted member samples in the column of “P_mem”.

In general, The White-box-Local MIA-FL achieves similar performance to the Grey-box II, while the White-box-Agg MIA-FL is able to obtain more targeted membership information, which aligns with the performance in the non-targeted MIA-FL.

RQ2: Performance of MIA-FL against robust aggregation

Table V. Performance of MIA-FL against robust aggregation mechanisms

Context in paper

In this section, we investigate the performance of MIA- FL against robust aggregation mechanisms, which are the main countermeasure against training data manipulation. We consider the following mechanisms as they have been widely discussed in the literature.

  • Krum and Multi-krum Krum and Multi-krum compare participants′ local updates based on the distance, and select the top one (Krum) and top N (Multi-krum) trustworthy updates before aggregating them.

  • Trimmed-mean and Median Both of them are averaging-based mechanisms, i.e., aggregates each dimension of input gradients separately using the mean or median values.

  • Fang defenses It detects and filters out potentially malicious local updates based on their impact on the error rate (Error Rate based Rejection) and the loss (Loss Function based Rejection).

We summarize the performance of MIA-FL against the robust aggregation mechanisms in Table V. In the paper, we report our results on CIFAR-10 and Location30, which represents a well-generalized and a less-generalized model respectively. More results on other datasets can be found at our online repository. On average, the robust aggregation mechanisms decreases 4.78%, 8.28%, and 10.52% of attack accuracy in the settings of Black-box, Grey-box-I, White- box-Local, and 6.25%, 9.47% on CIFAR-10 and Location30 respectively.

Optimized MIA-FL

Table VI. Performance of optimized MIA-FL against robust aggregation mechanisms

Context in paper

In this section, we evaluate the optimized MIA-FL. We focuse on answering the following RQ that is a follow-up question of RQ2.

  • RQ3. How much improvement can the optimized MIA-FL achieve when the FL is employed with robust aggregation mechanisms?

We select those aggregation mechanisms which are effective to MIA-FL (see Section VI-B), including Krum, Multi-krum and Fang defenses. For Krum, because of its stickiness to a certain candidate (as discussed in Section VI-B), we find that with the optimized MIA-FL, only the attacker′s updates are selected by Krum throughout the whole training process. Such results destroy the entire FL training and also make the attack setting invalid, since all the member samples submitted to the training is from the attacker. We therefore only report the performance of the optimized MIA-FL against Multi-krum and Fang defenses in Table VI and Figure 5. In the Black-box setting with Multi-Krum on CIFAR10, Fang defenses on CIFAR10 and Fang defenses on Location30 (denoted with “--” in Table VI), we do not conduct optimized MIA-FL because the robust aggregation mechanisms only reduce less than 2% of attack accuracy from the baseline (see Table II).
